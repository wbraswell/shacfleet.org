Thoughts on Sea Scout Advancement
  1. Unless specifically stated otherwise, Sea Scout advancement requirements may be met at any time.  For instance, passing the Ordinary Marlinspike Seamanship requirements may be done before completing Apprentice.  Passing Able Boat Handling may be accomplished as Apprentice or Ordinary.
  2. There is not a “time in grade” requirement to any rank, per se.  A youth may earn Apprentice and Ordinary at the same time, as long as both sets of requirements are met.  They have to be Ordinary long enough to complete the Long Cruise.  In an extreme case, that is 15 days.  They have to be Able long enough to complete their service project and possibly their Quartermaster cruise.  Again, only a couple weeks in an extreme case.
  3. The 75% attendance for six months, one year and eighteen months may be counted from joining Sea Scouts.  Each ship should define what acceptable attendance for the 75% is so that everyone knows the ground rules.  Two examples are provided:

a. Ship 000 has weekly meeting (26 in 6 months) and monthly activities (6 in 6 months), totaling 32.  If a youth attends 24 meetings/activities in 6 months, s/he meets the attendance requirement.  If we schedule an additional work day or other activity, the youth will get credit toward the 24 by going, but will not be penalized if s/he does not attend.

b. In any given month, Ship 000 normally schedules four meetings and an activity.  Attendance at four of the five is 80%.  A youth may attend in April, June, August, September, October and December, and have attended 75% of the meetings in six months (just non-consecutive).  Because of many outside activities, family vacations, school conflicts, etc., this is an alternative way to count attendance.  It will take longer, but the youth are attending.

c. Either option A or option B is acceptable.

d. Quartermaster attendance (18 months) can be completed in 18 calendar months, while the individual is still only an Ordinary or even Apprentice.

  1. APP 2b – Define which uniform(s) are necessary to meet this requirement.  The traditional chambray, the white uniform, or the 21st Century blue uniform with appropriate insignia and headgear.
  2. Quarterdeck training (ORD 3a) should be defined by the ship.  The crew officers’ seminar on page 41 of the Venturing Leader Manual is good.  Alternatives to the COS are the Boy Scout JLTC, or the Introduction to Leadership Skills Ships, or other equivalent courses.  Usually, these require at least one day of interactive training.  Some take the point of view that Quarterdeck training is what is done right after the election, but it does not exclude younger Sea Scouts who are not part of the Quarterdeck. 
  3. Sea Scouts are required to complete their state’s NASBLA approved boater safety course (the TPWD Boater Safety course in Texas) or USPS equivalent (www.tpwd.state.tx.us, Boating, Boater Education, On-Line Course) as part of Ordinary.  The Small Boat Handler Bar should be awarded on completion of the course and the Apprentice knots.
  4.  Work (APP 8 & ORD 15) projects should be pre-approved by the skipper.  However, certain work should be defined by the ship as pre-approved or expected to be approved.  Examples may be:  work on our ship's or fleet boats, service projects for the sponsoring institution or associated with Scouting, activity project manager preparation time (limited to 1-2 hours).  Maintenance work performed during outings may be counted, but only the time involved in the work.  Work on special work-only days may be counted “portal-to-portal.”  Normal ship outings, work for other charitable organizations, etc., do not normally count.
  5. Completion of the Swimming merit badge anytime after age 14 (or 13 and completion of the 8th grade) shall be considered completion of Apprentice requirement 4 and Ordinary requirement 4.  Ships may decide that completion of the Lifesaving merit badge any time after age 14 (or 13 and completion of the 8th grade) shall be considered completion of Apprentice requirement 4, Ordinary requirement 4, and Able requirement 4.  Quartermaster candidates too young to obtain Red Cross certification may still count the Red Cross lifesaving course toward Quartermaster if they pass it.  Sea Scouts must be 15 to complete Scout Lifeguard.  As a practical matter, Sea Scouts should be aware that completion of the American Red Cross or YMCA Lifesaving courses are more likely to lead to a summer lifeguard job than completion of the Scout Lifeguard course.
  6. Completion of the First Aid merit badge after age 14 or American Red Cross/American Heart Association Standard First Aid and CPR certification after age 14 shall be considered completion of Able requirement 5fgh.
  7. ABLE 13 requires a long cruise.  The long cruise badge may be earned without actually going on a two-week or one-week cruise.  Earning the Long Cruise badge is sufficient.  The critical item in logging long cruise days is that they must be connected by a night.  A day sail does not count.
  8. Members may hold membership in other Scout troops (boy or girl), Venturing crews or other ships.  Set a policy on how to accomplish advancement requirements in cooperation with those other units.  Some ships will accept that advancement when the Skipper is satisfied that the ship's standards of accomplishment were met.
  9. Sea Scouts expects youth to be competent in each skill to advance.  Memorizing a skill and passing it on the same day is not acceptable.  Youth are expected to be able to do the skill without preparation a week or two later.  Adults need to understand that unused skills are lost.
  10. Youth are expected to be part of the advancement process.  An Apprentice youth is expected to be able to teach and pass others on the Apprentice requirements, Ordinary on Ordinary and Apprentice, etc.  Consult the Skipper or a mate for approval to pass others on Sea Scout requirements.  If the youth is unsure about standards, consult an adult.  If the youth is unsure about their own competence, they are encouraged to review and prepare to be able to help the other Sea Scout, with the aid of someone more expert.  An important part of the learning experience is to be able to teach the skill to others.  When youth are competently teaching Apprentice, Ordinary and/or Able skills, those that count toward Quartermaster should be credited.
  11. Family should not pass youth on Sea Scout requirements, unless no one else has the skills to do so.
  12. Rank Requirements:  It is the responsibility of the Sea Scout to inform the Skipper that they are interested in advancement, and it is also the Sea Scout’s responsibility to request some time or an appointment with the Skipper/mate/committeeman/ship’s officer to be able to review rank requirements and get credit for passing them.  However, the adult leaders and youth should pro-actively assist youth in advancing.  There are many opportunities for “stealth advancement.”  When a Sea Scout goes forward to lower the anchor, and does it correctly, s/he should get credit toward that portion of the advancement requirement.  When it is time to raise the anchor, done correctly, then give credit again.  While it may be necessary to schedule anchoring so youth can pass the test, if you anchor for another reason it is still an opportunity to complete the requirements.
  13. Adults are able to grant credit for passing rank requirements once they themselves have learned how to do the skills, been able to use and demonstrate the skills.  Adults are additional resources to help youth pass the requirements.  However, they should not interfere with the youth-teaching-youth learning process.
  14.  Note:  According to the BSA Scoutmaster’s Handbook, a scout must be able to demonstrate that they have “mastered” the skills, before they are given credit for passing a rank requirement.  The four steps in advancement are “A Scout learns, is tested, is reviewed and then is recognized”.   (In psychology, “learning” does not take place unless “behavior modification can be detected”.)  The steps involved in “learning” include: the Scout is taught (or reads it), the Scout demonstrates, then the Scout applies.
  15. Sea Scouts may earn all Venturing Awards.  Each Sea Scout who completes the Ordinary rank has automatically earned the Sea Scout Bronze award in Venturing.  Sea Scouts should be encouraged to qualify for the Gold and Silver awards in Venturing, and for other Venturing awards that are available, as well as Hornaday, and the aquatics specialty badges such as board sailing, SCUBA, etc.  Sea Scouts may earn any of the other Bronze Awards, or the Ranger, Quest, or TRUST awards.
  16. After several years of dealing with Sea Scout advancement, the following ideas are recommended:

a. Make a six-month (or longer) ship plan.  Build up to some kind of superactivity (long cruise) that will keep the ship's youth focused. 

b. Determine what skills are needed to execute the activity.  Teach those skills during your meetings and activities leading up to your major activity. 

c.  Check those skills off as they are learned and applied.  Recycle where needed.  Look for other skills (requirements) that are learned while your main process is going on.  Check those other skills off as well.

d. Some things are just learned as part of the process.  Able 7b, Boat Handling, requires the youth demonstrate line handling and docking commands.  We don't consciously teach this requirement, we just “do” it, every time we go sailing.  After a youth is Ordinary, and has about half the work done toward Able, we will be going through her requirements one day, and say, “What do you know about line handling?” The youth will be able to explain what to do, and the commands, and I will have observed the accomplishments, and I will sign it off.  No formal test, I know the youth has met our objectives and realizes it also.

e. Encourage the older youth to teach and pass the younger youth.  When they come back and say that Sam has passed tying a Marline Hitch in Ordinary Marlinspike, record it right then.  Both the passee and passer get immediate feedback that they are competent and trusted. 

f.  Record every smaller step that is accomplished.  Don't insist that the youth pass all of Ordinary Marlinspike before you record it.  Many recording systems only break it down that fine.  Under separate cover, we provide two different scoresheets that break down the requirements for every rank into smaller sub-steps, which allow for recording “partials.”  As with merit badges, there is no time limit.  As the youth sees fewer and fewer blank spaces, it concentrates their mind on the next task to accomplish.  You may record directly in the youth's book.  You may record on a sheet of paper for the youth to keep.  Many personally keep a record for themselves, so they can see what is most needed by the youth for each rank.  Ship 1996 keeps the record electronically as well as on paper, and periodically sends it around to youth and parents for their information.

George Crowl, Skipper SSS Defiant, 1996

May 2013
