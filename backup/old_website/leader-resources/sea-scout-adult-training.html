Training - Adults

BSA Sea Scout Adult Training
Requirements and Recommendations

The following describes all the appropriate training for Sea Scout leaders.  There are other trainings that are useful, but these are specific to Sea Scout leadership.  All of the trainings below should be recorded with the ship's adult training monitor.  One person in the ship MUST have some of the trainings below, but in general all leaders actively working on the water should take the training below.

To be “BSA Trained”:

1.   Venturing Youth Protection Training (YPT, Y02) (required on registration in Venturing/Sea Scouts and every two years thereafter).

a.   Available on-line at MyScouting.org.  Link your BSA number to your training account.

2.   Sea Scout Adult Leader Basic Training (SSALBT, P44)

a.   The nuts and bolts of organizing, running, and growing the Sea Scout program – people, resources, advancement, etc.

b.   Taught face-to-face.  PPT available online.  SHAC has many qualified trainers.

c.   Every Skipper, mate and 2/3 of the other registered leaders in the ship are expected to take this to get Journey to Excellence Silver in Training.

BSA required to lead water activities and file a tour plan:

3.   Safety Afloat (every two years)

a.   Take online at MyScouting.org.

b.   Take before conducting a boating activity.

c.   Your youth need to take this training for Ordinary advancement.

4.   Safe Swim Defense (every two years)

a.   Take online at MyScouting.org.

b.   Take before conducting a boating activity.

c.   Your youth need to take this training for Apprentice advancement.

5.   Weather Hazard Training (every two years)

a.   Take online at MyScouting.org.

b.   Take before conducting any activity.

6.   Trek Safely (every two years, not required for routine activities)

a.   Take online at MyScouting.org.

b.   Take before conducting a long cruise or extensive trek.

7.   American Red Cross or American Heart Association First Aid (every two years)

8.   American Red Cross or American Heart Association CPR (adult, youth and infant, every 1 to 2 years)

9.   American Red Cross  or American Heart Association AED (every 1 to 2 years)

a.   Take these three courses (usually all at the same time) before going on an activity.

b.   Your youth need to take this training for Able advancement.  The ship should offer it every year.

Required by BSA:

10.       NASBLA approved state boater course (once) (BSA course code P47)

a.   Available online in most states. 

b.   Available from USPS and USCGA in most states.  Google for face-to-face schedule.

c.   Required to earn the Scouter's Key and Scouter's Training Award.

d.   Many councils require this course before you can assume command of a boat with Sea Scouts in it.  It is required for Sea Scouts to command a boat in many states.

Technical boating training (not required by the BSA, but highly recommended):

11.       Technical boating training is offered by the United States Power Squadron (USPS) and the United States Coast Guard Auxiliary (USCGA).  We strongly recommend that you meet the skill standards of one of these two organizations, or that of a Coast Guard licensed deck officer.  Once.

a.   United States Power Squadrons. USPS offers many courses.  The key ones are:

1)   NASBLA approved course mentioned above under the title “America's Boating Course.”  (BSA course code P47)

2)   Seamanship.  (BSA course code P45)  Introduction of many seamanship topics, expands on the NASBLA course content.

3)   Sailing.  No BSA course code.  Teaches the basics of sailing terminology, procedures and techniques.

4)   Piloting.  (BSA course code P46)  Teaches chart reading, position plotting, dead reckoning, and GPS navigation.

5)   USPS offers Advanced Piloting and several offshore navigation courses that include celestial.  They also offer many two-hour seminars on specific topics that will be of use to Sea Scouts and leaders.  See http://www.usps.org/e_stuff/public_ed.htm.

b.   United States Coast Guard Auxiliary. USCGA offers several courses.  Their format is somewhat different than USPS.

1)   NASBLA approved course mentioned above under the title “About Boating Safety.” (BSA course code P47)

2)   Boating Skills and Seamanship.  Designed for power boaters.  How to handle a power boat and many basic seamanship topics.  Expands on the NASBLA content.  (BSA course code P45)

3)   Sailing Skills and Seamanship.  Designed for sail boaters.  How to handle a sailboat, terminology, procedures, techniques.  Expands on the NASBLA content.  (Also BSA course code P45)

4)   Weekend Navigator.  Piloting topics based on chart reading, position plotting, dead reckoning, and GPS navigation.  (BSA course code P46)

5)   Several other courses, mostly 3-4 hours in length, on a variety of topics.  See http://cgaux.org/boatinged/ .

c.   United States Coast Guard. Many schools offer a Coast Guard captain's license.  The “Six-Pack” license usually takes 40 hours or so of instruction.  Any Coast Guard license will qualify a Scouter for P47, P45 and P46.  Scouters who operate a vessel requiring a license must acquire that license before operating in command.

12.       American Red Cross Wilderness and Remote First Aid.  (Two years) Designed to train youth and adults to respond to situations where first responders may be several hours or miles away and you must take the injured person to treatment.  Appropriate if you may be on a boat that must travel to reach a first responder.

BSA Advanced Training:

BSA advanced training is not required, but is recommended.  It provides a deeper understanding of our purposes, procedures and techniques.  This training may take several days or a week and may require travel to attend a course.  Courses may only be offered a few times a year in the whole country.

13.       Wood Badge.  This world-wide leadership training course has roots provided by Baden-Powell 95 years ago.  It uses the latest leadership techniques to provide you tools to use in Scouting and in your professional and personal life to do a better job and get along better with your co-workers.  It can change your life!  Six days, either consecutive or two weekends.  Requires a “ticket” that provides practical experience in the skills taught.

14.       Seabadge.  This is a weekend conference, normally starting Friday evening and ending Sunday noon.  It is an advanced discussion of the leadership and administrative aspects of Sea Scouts.  It does not discuss seamanship at all.  Recommended for all Skippers, mates, and committee chairs.

15.       Seabadge Underway.  This is a three full day or two weekend practical leadership laboratory typically using a 40-foot power or sail boat.  Participants function as Sea Scout youth with instructors in the adult role to see how the ideal Sea Scout ship should operate.  Participants must already have the Ordinary and most of the Able Piloting skills, they are not taught, you are assumed to have them. You must pass a test on seamanship and a test on piloting.

16.       Powder Horn.  Provides an introduction to high-adventure activities.  Identifies that no leader can be an expert in all high-adventure activities, so provides lists of local resources who can lead activities that are not of your own expertise.  Provides a tool kit of how to find consultants to lead the activities your Scouts want to go on.
